Modal Verbs - Complete Guide

Learn how to use modal verbs with examples

What are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs are helper verbs that express ability, possibility, permission, necessity, or advice. Common modals include: can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would.

Tips and Tricks for Modal Verbs

  • No 's' ending for modals: Modal verbs never change form for he, she, or it.
    ✅ Correct: She can go. He should study.
    ❌ Incorrect: She cans go. He shoulds study.
  • Modal + base verb (without ‘to’): Always use the base form of the verb after a modal verb.
    ✅ Correct: I can swim. You should try.
    ❗ Exception: Use ‘to’ only with ought to and have to.
  • Making negatives: Add not directly after the modal.
    ✅ She should not go.
    ✅ He cannot play.
    Contracted forms: shouldn’t, can’t, won’t.
  • Questions with modals: Structure → Modal + subject + base verb.
    Example: Can you help? Should she come?
  • Past modal forms: Use modal + have + past participle for past possibilities or regrets.
    Example: She should have studied harder. He could have won the race.

Quick Summary Table

Modal Use Example
Can Ability/Permission I can swim.

Examples of Each Modal Verb

1. Can

I can swim. (Ability)
You can leave now. (Permission)

2. Could

I could run fast when I was younger. (Past ability)
Could you help me, please? (Polite request)

3. May

You may go now. (Permission)
It may rain today. (Possibility)

4. Might

She might come to the party. (Possibility)

5. Must

You must wear a helmet. (Obligation)
He must be tired. (Logical conclusion)

6. Should

You should study for the exam. (Advice)
She should see a doctor. (Suggestion)

7. Will

I will call you tomorrow. (Future)
He will help you. (Promise)

8. Would

Would you like some tea? (Polite request)
If I were rich, I would travel. (Hypothetical)
